01
Markets are materially underpricing the Iran conflict
Equities and credit have priced in only 35% of the S&P 500 drawdown and 27% of the leveraged loan spread widening seen during Liberation Day. Inflation breakevens reflect just 67% of the Ukraine invasion response. KKR believes this optimism is misplaced — supply chains were already tight before the Hormuz closure, and the disruption is already rippling through Asia. Betting markets show only a 30% probability of a U.S.–Iran ceasefire in April.
High Conviction Risk
02
Oil becomes the dominant macro variable — base case $90–100 WTI
KKR elevates its 60% base case to $90–100 WTI ($100–110 Brent) in 2026, with peak months in the mid-$100s. The prior base of $75–80 becomes the 15% low scenario. The 25% high case — Hormuz restrictions plus Gulf infrastructure damage — implies an average of $130 with peaks near $180 (Brent +$200). Beyond 2026, WTI is expected to average $75–80, $5–10 above the forward curve, reflecting a persistent geopolitical risk premium.
Key Watch Variable
03
Growth headwinds accelerate faster than historical models predict
U.S. GDP is cut to 2.0% in 2026 and 1.6% in 2027, both below consensus. Unlike typical demand-driven energy shocks, this is a pure supply shock — KKR expects the energy "tax" on disposable incomes to weigh on growth as soon as Q2 2026, versus the typical 4–6 quarter lag. Discretionary consumption faces acute pressure: the lowest income quintile spends 17.4% of pre-tax income on energy, versus just 2.7% for the highest quintile.
Growth Warning
04
Private Credit: recovery rates, not defaults, define this cycle
Fitch's broad default measure — including PIK and amend-and-extend — has run at 5–6% for two years and is trending up to 5.6%. KKR expects recovery rates, not headline defaults, to define outcomes. Historical recovery rates of 50–60% could fall to 30–40% for smaller companies with loose deal terms or where lenders are unwilling to take over. Endgame winners will originate well, construct thoughtfully, and actively manage underperformers.
Portfolio Lens
05
Regime Change is real — traditional 60/40 portfolio construction is failing
Since 2020, four forces have been reshaping the macro backdrop: larger fiscal deficits, heightened geopolitical uncertainty, a messy energy transition, and stickier, more volatile inflation. The Iran conflict reinforces all four. Stocks and bonds are now positively correlated, eroding traditional diversification. The 5-year efficient frontier has narrowed — the return differential between best and worst assets is now 7.4%, down from 9.1% a few years ago. KKR recommends leaning into Private Markets: operational alpha in PE, upfront yield in Private Credit, and inflation-linked cashflows in Infrastructure and Asset-Based Finance.
Structural Macro Thesis
KKR Core Thesis — April 2026
"We subscribe to the muddle through thesis — markets are digesting geopolitics, a re-fueled inflation debate, private credit normalization, and AI's unresolved impact on growth and employment all at once. Oil is the fastest transmission channel through which geopolitics can alter the path for inflation, growth, and central bank policy."

Regime Change & Portfolio Construction

Since 2020, four forces have been reshaping the macro backdrop. The Middle East conflict reinforces all four and continues to erode the effectiveness of traditional 60/40 portfolio construction

📊
Larger Fiscal Deficits
U.S. deficit widens from 2.3% to 6.1% of GDP. Germany from −1.3% to 3.6%. UK from 3.8% to 4.7%. China from 4.9% to 8.1%. All 2019 to 2026e.
⚡
Heightened Geopolitical Uncertainty
Iran conflict intersects with Taiwan semiconductor risk, ongoing trade friction, and a blurring of economics, rule of law, and national security via technology and data.
🌍
Messy Energy Transition
Supply shocks reinforce the need for energy security and diversified sources. CEOs demand more supply chain resiliency. "Security of Everything" capex thesis intensifies.
📈
Stickier, More Volatile Inflation
Inflation remains structurally elevated 2026–27. Headline volatility driven by energy. Stock–bond correlation turns positive, undermining 60/40 as reliable shock absorber.

Portfolio Construction Implications
KKR's recommended positioning in a narrower-return, higher-volatility world
Private Equity
Operational alpha with less reliance on leverage. Companies able to streamline footprints and sell non-core assets will outperform in an environment of uneven growth.
Private Credit
Upfront yield and stronger structures. Focus on origination quality and active portfolio management. Recovery rate outcomes will differentiate manager performance this cycle.
Infrastructure & Asset-Based Finance
Inflation-linked cash flows with contractual frameworks that dampen macro noise. Security of Everything and energy security spending drive ongoing capex across power, logistics, and defense.
